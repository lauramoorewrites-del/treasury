The *Saga of the Sons of Droplaug* embeds an authority claim for the oral teller, saying that the saga (and the events therein) were first told by a descendant of a principal character. It's like the concept of a chain of custody for legal evidence: proof that the saga is reliable because of evidence of its handling since the events in question.

> A further reference used to argue the case for the sagas of Icelanders having existed in oral form occurs at the end of Droplaugarsona saga ('Saga of the Sons of Droplaug'), where it is said that the saga was told by a descendant of one of the main characters, Grímr Droplaugarson.

— Gísli Sigurðsson, [*The Medieval Icelandic Saga and Oral Tradition*](/treasury/reference-works-on-oral-storytelling-tradition/) (Harvard University Press, 2004), p. 36.

On narrative perspective:

> The narrative perspective in the [Icelandic] sagas is almost invariably restricted to what somebody could have seen and then told to others.

— Sigurðsson, p. 37.

An interesting distinction is drawn between how the sagas' immediate audiences conceived of them ("as conveying reliable information about the past") and how Medieval people, the ones who produced the text versions we have today, would have viewed their object ("as their attempt to create a particular image of a past"). The conversion from oral to written happened alongside a conversion from a belief in the historical nature of the texts to a belief that they only created a "particular image" of historical times, one that is not necessarily true.

> Rather than ask whether the sagas are history or fiction, he attempts to approach them from the perspective of what people in the Middle Ages would themselves have thought — as their attempt to create a particular image of a past which he associates with social changes that occurred first with the coming of Christianity and then with loss of independence to the king of Norway in 1262. The sagas themselves are subject to artistic laws and are not to be regarded as descriptions of reality, even if they are written as part of a tradition that their immediate audiences would in all probability have viewed as conveying reliable information about the past.

— Sigurðsson, p. 41.
