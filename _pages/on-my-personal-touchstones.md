title: "2026-06-23 On my personal touchstones"
---

Every so often, I encounter an idea that embeds in my mind and becomes a durable hinge for my thinking. Maybe hinge isn't quite right. The idea becomes a filter, or lens, or explanatory mechanism that I can apply to understanding something *else* better. I've started to think of these as personal touchstones: things I return to, touch back on, use as points of orientation.

What's an example?

Donna Haraway's "god trick." I spoke about it [in this essay](https://www.strangeclarity.com/p/the-god-trick-and-how-we-read-authority), but the underlying work of philosophy deserves in-depth treatment; maybe I'll attempt that sometime. 

From a nice [profile](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2019/jun/20/donna-haraway-interview-cyborg-manifesto-post-truth): "Haraway was part of an influential cohort of feminist scholars who trained as scientists before turning to the philosophy of science in order to investigate how beliefs about gender shaped the production of knowledge about nature."

She identified a vocal register where the speaker purports to escape and transcend any limits on his power to know and to judge -- adopting a "view from above."

These speakers "claim the power to see and not be seen, to repre­sent while escaping representation." They can pronounce criticism, but not be criticized themselves. They assume an infinite vision, one unblocked by barriers or shadows or partial sight. "But, of course, that view of infinite vision is an illusion, a god trick."

This is a trick I've encountered often in my life, but didn't have the tools to criticize -- until Haraway offered one. Now I can examine a text with Haraway's voice asserting itself: is the speaker pretending to unfettered vision, or are they situating their knowledge by acknowledging their particular point of view? 

Here, *point of view* is used more literally than we're accustomed to. Often it's used interchangeably with *opinion*, but one's *point of view* is upstream of opinions and judgments. It goes some way to explain how and why those opinions and judgments are what they are.
