Hildegard of Bingen lived in the *Fraunenklause*, the women's hermitage or anchorhold, at the monatsery at Disibodenberg from 1112 (per the *Life of Jutta* discovered in 1999) until she established a new convent at Rupertsberg in 1150.

Disibodenberg sits on a forested hilltop at the confluence of the Glan and Nahe rivers, near Odernheim am Glan in Rhineland-Palatinate. A monastic settlement existed there from the 7th century (associated with the Irish missionary St. Disibod); the Benedictine refoundation under Archbishop Ruthard of Mainz dates to 1108. The site was suppressed in the Reformation, the buildings progressively ruined, and the dressed stone quarried over the following centuries.

The authoritative modern archaeological campaign is the 1985–1989 excavation under Dr. Günter Stanzl for the Landesdenkmalpflege Rheinland-Pfalz, which produced the site plan and interpretation still in use. The site is now an archaeological park maintained by the Disibodenberger Scivias-Stiftung, with a numbered visitor tour (*Rundgang*) keyed to surviving features.

The archaeological findings indicate the following about the structure of the anchorhold:

### Phase 1: First Frauenklause (site tour Nr. 14b), 1112 – late 1130s

The structure where Jutta, Hildegard, and a third companion (also named Jutta) were enclosed on All Souls Day, 1 November 1112, and where Jutta died in 1136. It was an annex built against the foundations of the oldest church on the mountain.

The original space was small and, at the time of Jutta's death, had grown to about 10 disciples, and there would have been servants as well.

### Phase 2: Second Frauenklause + lay chapel (site tour Nr. 17), late 1130s onward

After Jutta's death, when Hildegard became magistra, the anchorhold was expanded. It featured a chapel of the women's own, a residential tower, and a preserved window through which the enclosed women could observe chapel services without being seen.
