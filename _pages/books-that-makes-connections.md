I like books that make connections, whose point seems to largely *be* about making connections.

Rebecca Solnit’s *The Faraway Nearby* takes us through a series of unexpected connections: Mary Shelley, Frankenstein’s monster, leprosy, Che Guevara, the Tang Dynasty artist Wu Daozi.

*Doppelganger*, by Naomi Klein, was a transformative reading experience for just this reason: it was the first book I read that piled association on association, that sought to explain Klein’s personal experience by bridging outward to film, literature, history, politics. In the months after I read it, I told everyone else to read it — if you had a conversation with me then, I tried to push the book on you.

My habit is to read reviews of a thing *after* I consume it — film, TV shows, books. I don’t need to know what someone else thought before I form my own thoughts, but when I enjoy something, I want to talk about it with others, and usually the only way to do that is to search out reviews for asynchronous communion. 

In that vein, I was lapping up an excellent *Public Books* review of *The Faraway Nearby* by B.R. Cohen. And embedded within was a gift: a recommendation for Rachel Cohen’s *A Chance Meeting*:

> ...one of my favorite books. Like *The Faraway Nearby*, it’s about connections and webs of life, although *A Chance Meeting* is not a memoir but a repository of threaded lives, showing the separate lives of famous writers and thinkers converging in various episodes. Gertrude Stein and William James. William James and W. E. B. DuBois. DuBois and Charlie Chaplin. Chaplin and Hart Crane. Crane and Katherine Anne Porter.

Oh yes. Adding that to the list.

B.R. Cohen sounds like a kindred spirit, “unusually attuned to seeing connections between things, ideas, people.” Funny, he was worried about this trait in himself — something I’ve never felt about my own connection-making. I see it as a precious facility, not a burden. A difference, though: he apparently spun webs of connections for his away-from-keyboard friends, who, it sounds like, (im)patiently waited for him to simply finish. (“It irritated friends when I’d go on about these overlapping metaphors.”) I save my connection-making for an internet audience.
