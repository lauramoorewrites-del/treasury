title: "Ira Glass on taste killing motivation"
---

In pure commonplace book spirit, I’m saving this wonderfully explicatory quotation by Ira Glass on how an abundance of taste can kill motivation when you’re just starting out.

> Nobody tells this to people who are beginners; I wish someone told me. All of us who do creative work, we get into it because we have good taste. But there is this gap. For the first couple years you make stuff, it’s just not that good. It’s trying to be good, it has potential, but it’s not. But your taste, the thing that got you into the game, is still killer. And your taste is why your work disappoints you.
> 
> A lot of people never get past this phase, they quit. Most people I know who do interesting, creative work went through years of this. We know our work doesn’t have this special thing that we want it to have. We all go through this. And if you are just starting out or you are still in this phase, you gotta know it’s normal and the most important thing you can do is do a lot of work.
> 
> Put yourself on a deadline so that every week you will finish one story. It is only by going through a volume of work that you will close that gap, and your work will be as good as your ambitions. And I took longer to figure out how to do this than anyone I’ve ever met. It’s gonna take a while. It’s normal to take a while. You’ve just gotta fight your way through.

My tentative correction is to that last paragraph. I’m not sure the one-story-a-week technique is enough in itself. That *volume* is enough in itself. You could phone in a story a week and not make much progress. You could force a story a week and not make much progress. What I’m saying is, I don’t think we have full control; it’s not sheer force of will. I think we need to be inspired, and then not waste that inspiration: we need to apply effort to process it and thereby acquire skill.

I’m not a fiction writer. I could force an empty story a week and still just be treading water, not progressing. I dunno, maybe that’s wrong, or partly off the mark. But I think there’s a kernel of truth to it.

The quotation was found in Richard Lemarchand’s *A Playful Production Process: For Game Designers (and Everyone)* (2021).
