Gil Morejón, [*The Unconscious of Thought in Leibniz, Spinoza, and Hume*](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/the-unconscious-of-thought-in-leibniz-spinoza-and-hume/7A9E83630FAB034CA86EFD4391B62497) (Edinburgh University Press, 2023).

From the publisher's synopsis: "These three early modern philosophers understood that minds necessarily involve ideas and patterns of thinking that are not conscious. Morejon shows that in this way they sharply distinguish themselves from other major early modern thinkers whose conceptions of the mind tended to identify thinking with consciousness, such as Descartes, Malebranche and Locke. This conception of the thinking mind as conscious remains popular even today. By contrast, Leibniz, Spinoza and Hume argue instead that thought is not, as such, a matter of consciousness."

Anything that's inaccurate or inartfully stated here is my clumsiness and should not be assumed to reflect on the book. ## Notes on the introduction

A good statement of Morejón's thesis: "I contend that a rational reconstruction of the philosophical systems of Leibniz, Spinoza, and Hume is in some way incomplete, or inadequate, if it is missing a concept of the unconscious of thought, since it plays a genuinely vital role in these systems."

To get out of the way, a clarifying question: why does Morejón say "unconscious of thought" rather than the smoother "unconscious thought"?

- I think he wants to emphasize the thought portion, and that thought itself has multiple dimensions (at least two, conscious and unconscious), rather than emphasize the unconscious aspect with its baggage of Freud and haphazardness. In a sense, he wants us to stumble a bit to break those associations.
- Still, semantically speaking, I think he could have simply said "unconscious thought" and made the same case, and I'm just going to say <i>unconscious thought</i> when speaking in my own words.

**(1)** Much of the Introduction defends a "so what?" by talking about akrasia.

Morejón believes that unconscious thought is the missing piece to explaining akrasia, a subject of philosophical investigation since Plato's *Protagoras*. Akrasia is the human propensity to act deliberately in a way that's contrary to the course of conduct they believe is best. For instance, a person might believe it's best to tell the truth in all cases, yet still tell lies. Or they might believe the best course is not to have a cup of coffee after dinner, but they do it on occasion all the same.

Surprisingly (to me), Socrates stated in *Protagoras* (this is Morejón's paraphrase) that "what one understands to be good is irresistibly desirable." So however we choose to act, it must follow that we believe that act to be the "good" choice. If we do something that is bad, then we are acting on imperfect information.

Morejón:

> I think a much better account of akratic desires and behaviours can be given on the basis of a conception of the mind and ideas that accords the unconscious dimension of thought its proper place, in which one's actions are significantly determined by unconscious elements of thought rather than only or primarily by conscious, voluntary decisions.

(Aside: As I read the Introduction, it occurs to me that you could frame the utility of many medicines as tackling akrasia. They bring our behavior more in line with what we've decided is good, but for some reason are unable to bring about on our own. Ozempic for eating less; ritalin for focusing better (as ADHD treatment); SSRIs for attaining day-to-day functionality (as depression treatment); medicines to treat the intrusive thoughts of OCD, etc.)

**(2)** Morejón uses the same metaphor I do (and I assume others do as well, is it instinctual?) when he says that the kind of thought he's describing is not "in consciousness but below or behind it." He repeatedly distinguishes the two kinds of thought spatially. (Elsewhere: "My determination takes place below and behind my consciousness of it.")

**(3)** Leibniz, Spinoza, and Hume do not talk about unconscious thought as such. Generally speaking, Morejón detects the concept of unconscious thought in Leibniz's "petites perceptions," Spinoza's "inadequate ideas and involuntary desire," and Hume's "involuntary and unconscious habits."

He cites Frank Tallis' *Hidden Minds: A History of the Unconscious* for the idea that an early grasp of the idea of the unconscious was in St. Augustine's "I do not grasp all that I am."

"Of course, none of the figures I treat have the word 'unconscious' in their vocabularies. The term itself seems to have been coined later, by F.W.J. Schelling, and several recent studies have traced the concept back to his work."

(Here, you might jump to: [Talking about a latent conceptual presence](/treasury/talking-about-a-latent-conceptual-presence/).)

**(4)** "Of my three main subjects, Leibniz can probably be said to come closest to directly pointing at the concept of the unconscious without yet naming it, by means of his famous distinction between apperception and perception, since this comes down to a difference between conscious and not-necessarily-conscious perception. Spinoza also comes close when in the Ethics he says that infants are 'as it were, unconscious of themselves [quasi sui inscius]'. Hume sometimes speaks of ideas of which we are 'insensible', even though they play a role in the operations of our mind."

**(5)** A key idea of Morejón (and this goes back to the "unconscious of thought" phrasing) is that the notion is not "merely negative." By this he means (I think), it's not just a conscious thought of which you're unaware — or, that the thought remains constant and all that changes is whether you're aware or unaware of it (i.e., conscious of it). Rather, the nature and structure of thought is different depending on whether it is unconscious or conscious. The "unconscious of thought" refers not just to the absence of consciousness, but a kind of entity with its own realm:

> In each case, we are not dealing with thoughts or ideas of which we merely happen not to be aware, but which are structurally and necessarily unconscious: perceptions too minute to register in the conscious mind, desires that are determined unconsciously by virtue of the unknown ideas that obscurely constitute them, associations and habits that are unconsciously formed in the very process of constituting a mind.

**(6)** For Morejón, consciousness is necessarily a product of unconscious thought: "At the same time, the unconscious of thought is always involved in a particular consciousness, as something that cannot but be expressed, even if this expression is obscure, incomplete, involuntary, or imperceptible."

**(7)** The remarks that close out the introduction bear on what this new philosophical understanding can be used for. The tone is optimistic: by understanding how unconscious thought directs our actions, in a way that conscious decision-making cannot, we might learn how to influence our unconscious thought. Morejón, in a fairly jargony formulation: "Along the way, this investigation might allow us to get a sense for what is necessary and sufficient for an ideal formation to be affectively powerful enough to produce real effects at the level of subjective determination. What is required is to develop the capacity of active thought to intervene in the processes of subjective genesis, which precede and subtend consciousness."
