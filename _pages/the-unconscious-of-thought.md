Gil Morejón, [*The Unconscious of Thought in Leibniz, Spinoza, and Hume*](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/the-unconscious-of-thought-in-leibniz-spinoza-and-hume/7A9E83630FAB034CA86EFD4391B62497) (Edinburgh University Press, 2023).

Morejón considers "the ways in which an unconscious element of thought has been conceived, prior to the coining of the term 'unconscious.'" He calls the philosophers' use of the as-yet-unnamed concept to bolster their explicit theories a case of "silent functioning."

> The set of readings presented here, then, seeks to distinguish between the real lack of a concept in certain figures and its merely apparent absence in others, in whom, however, the indispensable functioning of that very concept hums beneath the surface at almost every key point.

> Consciousness is, as Gilles Deleuze argues, an integration, "a matter of threshold": a synthesis in the present of complex disunities and impure mixtures, selections based on unclear principles and whose matter is obscure. It is ambivalently given at best; its genesis is, for us, a matter of inferential reconstruction and retrospective positing, never something immediately accessible.

> Our putative experience of this kind of freedom, for Spinoza, is in fact just a function of the epistemic gap between the real determining power of the unconscious of thought and our typically weak conscious grasp of the nature of things in their necessity.

Morejón cites Louis Althusser's *Reading Capital* (Maspero, 1965) for its discussion of "symptomatic reading," "in which the conspicuous absences of a text can bear as much significance as what it says outright."
