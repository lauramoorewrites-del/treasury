I contributed this Substack [Note](https://substack.com/@strangeclarity/note/c-278092972?r=emcf2&utm_source=notes-share-action&utm_medium=web) to a discussion of the curiosities of Biblical translation:

> On this, consider this line from Ecclesiastes 1:2: “_Vanity of vanities; all is vanity_.” That’s the King James Bible. The New Int’l Version translates it as: “_Utterly meaningless! Everything is meaningless._”
> 
> But the original Hebrew is less moralizing, and more philosophical. The key word is _hevel_, meaning breath, vapor, mist, something transient. The passage was translated from Hebrew into Greek, then from Greek into Latin, then from Latin into English. The more times a thing is reinterpreted through translation, the more its meaning drifts.
> 
> “_Everything is vapor_” doesn’t, to me, carry a moral charge (like _vanity_) or suggest despair (like _meaningless_). It reminds me of the Buddhist principle that nothing lasts; everything changes. But of course, here I’m importing my own subjective reading, which is probably the process that got us from _hevel_ to _vanity_ in the first place.

My original source was Rebecca Solnit's [*The Faraway Nearby*](https://bookshop.org/p/books/the-faraway-nearby-rebecca-solnit-ph-d/d2a94d49bca43794?ean=9780143125495) (2013), but on fact-checking her before endorsing the information I found it was partly wrong. Solnit writes that the original Hebrew word *hevel* was "unavailable until modern times, when scraps of surviving manuscript were found in dry caves." Apparently, that's not the case. 

The [Leningrad Codex](https://dornsife.usc.edu/wsrp/cylinder-seals-and-the-west-semitic-research-project/) is a Hebrew bible written around 1010 C.E. Through it, the Hebrew version of Ecclesiastes seems to have been continuously available to scholars; it was being used during the Middle Ages, for instance, to investigate ancient Hebrew pronunciations.

The biggest cause for the drift from *hevel* to *vanity* seems to have been [St. Jerome](https://www.logos.com/grow/meaning-of-vanity-in-ecclesiastes/), who used the Latin *vanitas*. That means *empty*, but not quite in the sense of *vain* or *shallow*. Still, the English *vanity* is a derivative of the Latin *vanitas*, and the rest is history. Something like that, at least.
