# Memory

The medieval conception of memory was beautifully tangible. They imagined our mental insides as a treasure box of knowledge entities accumulated and preserved over a lifetime — impressions stored like imprints of a stamp on wax, the raw material of the brain molded with miniatures of reality.

You could arrange these entities deliberately, traveling between them as though walking through the collection rooms of a museum. You'd store recollections of fear next to snakes, place related concepts in radial arrangements extending outward like a wheel's spokes from a central heading. That central heading is a *commonplace*, useful for launching extemporaneous speeches on a topic.

Medievals located genius in memory. Moderns locate it in imagination. Carruthers shows both eras describing identical working habits — Einstein and Aquinas — but crediting different faculties.
